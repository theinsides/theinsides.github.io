From the Editors

For five decades, the American sitcom has quietly perpetuated one of the most audacious economic frauds in entertainment history: characters of modest means living in implausibly lavish circumstances, their finances as fictional as the laugh tracks that once accompanied them.

The Sitconomist has expanded its forensic accounting to cover 37 iconic characters across six decades of American television. Using period-accurate wage data, city-specific rental indices, inflation adjustments to 2024 dollars, and conservative lifestyle-costing models, we have reconstructed full income-and-expenditure profiles for every subject.

Each character has been assigned an Implausibility Rating — our proprietary credit-style scoring system ranging from AAA (financially coherent) to D (economically catastrophic; should not exist as depicted). Click any character card to open a full financial dossier, complete with AI-generated editorial analysis, interactive scenario modelling, and charts. Adjust the variables. Run the numbers yourself. We warn you: the results are consistently, depressingly illuminating.

312% Average rent-to-income ratio

Across all 37 subjects, the mean proportion of declared income consumed by housing alone. The conventionally prudent ceiling is 30%. These characters observed no such ceiling, implicitly, for decades.

4 Financially defensible characters

Out of 37 subjects, only four demonstrate income-to-expenditure ratios that a competent accountant would not flag on sight. They are not who you would expect.
